# Platform Engineering for the AI-Native Enterprise

## A Playbook with Two Accelerators

> **Thesis.** Platform Engineering is the foundation layer for the AI-native enterprise. Every other capability the enterprise wants (context, cognition, intent, agents) compounds on top of that foundation or amplifies the friction underneath it. The fastest known way to build that foundation in 2026 is to adopt one of two opinionated accelerators on a shared Microsoft platform: **Three Horizons** for clients aligned with the Red Hat ecosystem, **Open Horizons** for clients optimizing for open-source neutrality. This playbook explains why, how to choose, and how to execute in 90 to 180 days.

## 1. What This Playbook Is

This is an enterprise playbook for Platform Engineering in the era of AI agents. It is not a product brochure, not a vendor pitch, and not a generic reference. It is the synthesis of three years of LATAM Software Global Black Belt engagements compressed into a single argument and a single execution sequence.

The argument is simple to state and difficult to dispute: in 2026, the rate of AI adoption has decisively exceeded the rate at which most enterprises can govern AI safely, and the gap is closed by Platform Engineering or it is not closed at all. The empirical record from DORA, MIT NANDA, Gartner, McKinsey, Forrester, IDC, and CNCF agrees on this. The execution sequence answers a more practical question: given that you must build the foundation, what is the fastest known path to do so without writing it from scratch?

The answer this playbook gives is that the fastest path is to adopt one of two opinionated, customer-deployed accelerators that materialize the foundation as code, then customize it. The two accelerators converge at the same destination but optimize for different starting positions.

**Three Horizons** is the path for clients with Red Hat investment, commercial-support requirements, or a vendor strategy that prefers a single throat to choke. It runs on Red Hat Developer Hub as the Internal Developer Platform, Azure Red Hat OpenShift as the cluster, OpenShift Pipelines and OpenShift GitOps for delivery, Red Hat Trusted Application Pipeline for supply chain, and OpenShift Service Mesh for runtime networking. Microsoft and Red Hat operate this stack as a joint strategic partnership.

**Open Horizons** is the path for clients optimizing for vendor neutrality, open-source posture, or strong internal Kubernetes upstream expertise. It runs on Backstage upstream as the Internal Developer Platform, Azure Kubernetes Service as the cluster, GitHub Actions plus Argo CD or Flux for delivery, GitHub Advanced Security plus SLSA and Sigstore for supply chain, and Istio or Linkerd at the client's choice for service mesh. Microsoft operates the foundation; the open-source community and integrators operate the upstream layers.

Both accelerators ride a shared Microsoft foundation that does not change between them: Azure as cloud, GitHub Enterprise for source control and DevOps, Entra Agent ID for agent identity, Microsoft Foundry for the AI platform, Microsoft Agent Framework for the agent runtime, Agent 365 for fleet lifecycle, and Authorization Fabric for cross-resource agent permissions. The accelerator changes; the foundation does not.

This playbook explains the thesis, the foundation, and the choice in eleven chapters. Read it from chapter 1 to chapter 10 as a single argument, or jump to the chapter that answers the question you have today using the reading paths in section 4.

## 2. Who Should Read This

The playbook is engineered for four overlapping audiences with different entry points.

**Platform Lead, Director of Platform Engineering, or Head of DevEx.** You are the operational owner of the foundation. You will read every chapter, but the chapters that matter most for your decisions are 03 (CNCF Four Pillars), 06 (Three Horizons), 07 (Open Horizons), 08 (Decision Framework), and 09 (Implementation Sequence). The playbook is structured so you can hand chapters 06 and 07 to your engineering team verbatim.

**VP of Engineering or CTO.** You are the strategic owner of the AI-native transformation. You will not read every chapter, but you cannot delegate the decisions in chapters 01 (Amplification Thesis), 02 (AI-Native Enterprise Pyramid), 04 (Failure Modes), 08 (Decision Framework), and 10 (Maturity, Measurement, ROI). These five chapters compose into a 90-minute exec brief that puts you in a position to sponsor the work credibly.

**Enterprise Architect, Staff Engineer, or Principal Engineer.** You are the technical conscience. You will read the entire playbook because you will be the one defending the architectural decisions in design reviews. The chapters with the densest engineering substance are 03 (Four Pillars), 06 (Three Horizons), 07 (Open Horizons), and 09 (Implementation Sequence). Chapters 01, 04, and 10 give you the evidence and the metrics to defend the work in front of finance and risk.

**Cloud or Security Architect responsible for Azure governance.** You own the foundation underneath both accelerators. Chapters 03 (Four Pillars), 06, and 07 cover the policy, identity, and supply-chain surface you must operate. Chapter 04 (Failure Modes) shows you the regression patterns you will be asked to prevent. Chapter 09 (Implementation Sequence) gives you the gates that your security review process must own.

A practical note. Every chapter is dense by deliberate choice (15-25 pages each). The reading paths in section 4 will save you time if your role does not require the full document.

## 5. The Core Thesis in One Page

If this playbook had to compress to a single page, this is what it would say.

In 2026, three things are simultaneously true. First, AI adoption is universal. Gartner expects 40 percent of enterprise applications to ship task-specific AI agents by 2026, up from less than 5 percent in 2024, and McKinsey's State of AI 2025 puts the share of organizations using AI in at least one function at 78 percent. Second, most AI projects fail in production. The MIT NANDA study reports a 95 percent pilot failure rate for enterprise generative AI projects, and Gartner predicts that 40 percent of agentic AI projects will be cancelled by the end of 2027. Third, the organizations that succeed share a structural property: they invested in Platform Engineering before they invested at scale in AI. DORA 2025 reports Internal Developer Platform adoption at 90 percent and finds that AI's productivity effect is conditional on the maturity of the underlying delivery system.

The synthesis is captured in a single line that has become the operative statement of the field, attributed to Chris Aniszczyk of CNCF at KubeCon EU 2026: *agents amplify what is good or bad in your ecosystem*. In weak systems, AI amplifies the friction (242.7 percent more incidents per pull request, per DORA 2025); in mature systems, AI compounds the capability (98 percent more pull requests merged, 20-30 percent productivity gains for elite performers). The difference between the two outcomes is the platform underneath.

The architecture is a five-layer pyramid. Platform Engineering at the base. Context Engineering above it. Cognitive Layer above that. Intent Engineering above the cognitive layer. Agentic Execution at the top. The stacking rule is non-negotiable: each layer can be retrofitted, but only in dependency order. Building agents on a missing foundation does not produce agents; it produces an amplified mess.

The control plane is the CNCF Four Pillars of Platform Control: Golden Paths, Guardrails, Safety Nets, and Manual Review Workflows. The 2026 reframing of those pillars treats them as the governance plane for AI agents, not just for human developers. Agents are first-class platform citizens; the four pillars govern them at platform speed.

The two accelerators are the fastest known way to materialize this foundation in 90 to 180 days rather than the industry baseline of 9 to 18 months. Three Horizons runs the foundation on Red Hat Developer Hub plus Azure Red Hat OpenShift, with Microsoft and Red Hat as joint commercial owners. Open Horizons runs the foundation on Backstage plus Azure Kubernetes Service, with Microsoft as commercial owner of the substrate and the open-source community owning the upstream. Both ride the same Microsoft foundation (Azure, GitHub Enterprise, Entra Agent ID, Microsoft Foundry, Microsoft Agent Framework). Both terminate at the same Horizon 3 destination.

The implementation sequence is 90 days to a credible foundation with three teams onboarded, 180 days to enhancement and context, 365 days to first production agent workloads governed by the platform's full control plane.

The single equation that summarizes the playbook:

> **Platform Engineering (foundation) + Three Horizons or Open Horizons (accelerator) + Microsoft Foundation (substrate) = AI-Native Enterprise (outcome).**

Build the foundation first, materialize it through one of the two accelerators, and the layers above it become tractable in months instead of years. Skip the foundation, and the layers above amplify the gap.

That is the playbook.

## 6. The Four Capability Pillars of Platform Engineering in 2026

The CNCF Platform Engineering Working Group's [Platform Maturity Model](https://tag-app-delivery.cncf.io/whitepapers/platform-eng-maturity-model/) codifies four pillars that every mature Internal Developer Platform must implement. In 2026 these pillars do double duty: they govern human-driven delivery, and re-expressed they govern agent-driven delivery. Chapter 03 covers each pillar in depth; this section gives the one-paragraph framing that orients the rest of the playbook.

**Golden Paths.** Opinionated, self-service workflows that take a developer or an agent from intent to a working, compliant system in minutes. Golden Paths are not templates; they are templates that encode the enterprise's current best practice for a specific outcome, versioned and improvable. In Three Horizons, Golden Paths are RHDH software templates. In Open Horizons, they are Backstage scaffolder templates. The Open Horizons accelerator ships 22 of them, organized by the Three Horizons maturity model: 6 H1 Foundation templates, 9 H2 Enhancement templates, 7 H3 Innovation templates.

**Guardrails.** Mechanisms that prevent non-compliant or unsafe configurations from being deployed in the first place. Preventive, not detective. In both accelerators, guardrails materialize as policy-as-code (Azure Policy, OPA Gatekeeper, or Kyverno), Workload Identity (replacing long-lived service credentials), signed-image admission (Sigstore plus admission controllers), and network security groups enforced at the platform edge.

**Safety Nets.** Detective and corrective mechanisms that recover from failures the guardrails did not prevent. GitOps reconciliation (Argo CD or Flux), observability stacks (Prometheus, Grafana, OpenTelemetry, Azure Monitor), backup and recovery (Velero), and progressive delivery (canary, blue-green, feature flags). Both accelerators ship four pre-configured Grafana dashboards covering platform health, cost, AI usage, and security.

**Manual Review Workflows.** Explicit human-in-the-loop checkpoints for decisions that should not be automated. In an AI-native platform, these checkpoints are *more*, not less, important than in a human-only platform, because automation now happens faster than human judgment can keep up unless judgment is deliberately inserted. Both accelerators implement manual review through environment-specific GitOps sync policies (dev auto, staging gated, production manual), Change Advisory workflows captured as GitHub Issues templates, and AI agent promotion gates that require review before an agent expands its permission scope.

The 2026 reframing makes the pillars the AI control plane: Golden Paths become the templates by which agents are instantiated, Guardrails become the policies agents must satisfy at runtime, Safety Nets become the reconciliation loops that correct agent drift, and Manual Review becomes the approval workflow for agent capability expansion. A platform that has codified the four pillars for human developers has most of what it needs to govern agents. A platform that has not, does not.

## 7. The Two Accelerators at a Glance

The full comparison lives in chapter 08. This section gives the one-table view.

| Dimension | Three Horizons (Red Hat) | Open Horizons (Open Source) |
|-----------|--------------------------|------------------------------|
| Internal Developer Platform | Red Hat Developer Hub (RHDH) | Backstage upstream |
| Cluster | Azure Red Hat OpenShift (ARO) | Azure Kubernetes Service (AKS) |
| Native CI/CD | OpenShift Pipelines (Tekton) | GitHub Actions |
| GitOps | OpenShift GitOps (Argo CD) | Argo CD or Flux |
| Policy as code | Red Hat Advanced Cluster Security + Azure Policy | OPA Gatekeeper or Kyverno + Azure Policy |
| Supply chain security | Red Hat Trusted Application Pipeline | GitHub Advanced Security + SLSA + Sigstore |
| Service mesh | OpenShift Service Mesh (Istio) | Istio or Linkerd |
| Support model | Red Hat Enterprise + Microsoft (joint strategic partnership) | Microsoft (Azure + GitHub) + community + integrators |
| AI assistance | Red Hat Lightspeed (as it matures) + GitHub Copilot | GitHub Copilot + Microsoft Foundry Agent Service |
| Commercial structure | Red Hat subscriptions + Azure consumption + GitHub Enterprise | Azure consumption + GitHub Enterprise (no Red Hat licensing) |
| Typical client profile | FSI, energy, government, healthcare, large state-owned | Digital natives, fintechs, modern retailers, Azure-native ISVs |
| Tilts toward this path when | Prior Red Hat investment, commercial-support requirements, "one throat to choke" preference | Vendor neutrality, OSS-first posture, strong upstream Kubernetes expertise |

The decision is rarely binary. In LATAM specifically, the typical pattern I see is that financial services and energy clients with on-premise Red Hat investments tilt toward Three Horizons; digital-native enterprises and fintechs tilt toward Open Horizons; and a third group sits in the middle and benefits from a one-day comparative discovery. Chapter 08 has the discovery method.

The critical property both accelerators share: **they terminate at the same Horizon 3 destination**. The harness components (golden paths, guardrails, sensors, evaluators, garbage collection agents, multi-agent orchestrators) map cleanly between the two stacks. The choice is a stack choice, not a strategy choice.

## 8. The Shared Microsoft Foundation

Underneath both accelerators sits a foundation that does not change. Naming the foundation up front prevents the most common procurement mistake: treating the IDP and cluster choices as if they implied different cloud, identity, or AI platforms. They do not.

| Layer | Components | Why It Is Shared |
|-------|------------|------------------|
| Cloud | Azure (LATAM regions with data residency: Brazil South, Brazil Southeast, Chile Central, Mexico Central) | The cluster type changes; the cloud does not. Both ARO and AKS are Azure services, governed by the same Azure subscription, identity, billing, and policy fabric. |
| Source control and DevOps | GitHub Enterprise Cloud with Advanced Security, Copilot Enterprise, Coding Agent | GitHub is the common source of truth, the common CI compute target, and the common policy boundary for both accelerators. |
| Identity | Microsoft Entra ID + Entra Agent ID | Both human and agent identities live in Entra. Agent fleet identity, scoped tokens, audit, revocation are uniform across both accelerators. |
| AI Platform | Microsoft Foundry (multi-model: OpenAI, Anthropic, Meta, Mistral) + Microsoft Agent Framework 1.0 | Foundry provides the model gateway, observability, and evaluation harness regardless of accelerator. Agent Framework provides the runtime. |
| Governance | Azure Policy, Defender for Cloud, Purview, Agent 365, Authorization Fabric | Cloud-level governance, agent fleet management, and cross-resource permission enforcement are uniform. |
| Observability | Azure Monitor, Application Insights, Foundry Observability | Logs, metrics, traces, and agent trajectories share the same telemetry plane. |
| LATAM Compliance | Aligned for LGPD, BACEN, ANEEL, Habeas Data, Ley 19.628, Ley 1581 | Sector-specific regulatory alignment is a foundation property, not an accelerator property. |

The implication for the procurement and architecture conversation is direct: the choice of accelerator is a choice of *developer platform layer*, not a choice of cloud, identity, AI provider, or compliance posture. That keeps the conversation honest and prevents the common failure mode where a platform decision is escalated into a cloud decision and never resolved.

## 10. How to Use This Playbook

Three concrete uses, each tested in client engagements.

**Use 1: Discovery brief.** Hand chapters 01, 02, 04, and 08 to a client before a discovery workshop. The four-chapter pre-read replaces a generic "what is platform engineering" deck and lets the workshop start with the decision instead of with the definition.

**Use 2: Architecture review.** Hand chapters 03, 06, and 07 to your platform team as the working substrate for an architecture review. The three chapters together specify the control plane, the Red Hat materialization, and the open-source materialization with enough density that the team can ratify or override the defaults explicitly.

**Use 3: Execution roadmap.** Hand chapter 09 to the program manager and the platform PM. The 90/180/365 sequence is engineered to be ratified, sized, and tracked without further synthesis. Chapter 10 gives the metrics the platform team will be measured by and the metrics the platform team must produce to be credible at the CFO level.

The playbook is not designed to be read once. It is designed to be the working substrate for the program for the first year. Update it as you learn. The version field in the YAML frontmatter exists for that reason.
