01FORCING FUNCTIONS · CHAPTER 01

Why modernize now

Mainframe and legacy modernization is no longer optional for most enterprises. The convergence of retiring COBOL expertise, regulatory deadlines, and AI-driven cost economics has compressed a decade-long question into a 24-36 month decision window.

The Brazilian COBOL estate

Brazil has one of the largest COBOL estates in Latin America, concentrated in regulated industries. Understanding this landscape is the starting point for any modernization plan.

Industry segmentCOBOL LOCKey systems
Commercial banks500M, 1BCore banking, payments, clearing
Development banks100M, 300MCredit, treasury, regulatory reporting
Insurance50M, 200MPolicy administration, claims, actuarial
Government200M, 500MTax, social services, payroll
Retail50M, 100MSupply chain, POS, ERP integrations

The total Brazilian COBOL estate is between 1.25B and 2.1B lines of code. Most enterprises sit at organizational maturity Level 0-1 (no agentic AI adoption or early experimentation). This creates both urgency and opportunity. Organizations that move first will establish a competitive advantage that compounds for years.

The workforce demographic

Brazil faces a 15% annual COBOL developer loss as experienced developers retire without replacement. Universities stopped teaching COBOL two decades ago. Junior engineers are not entering the field. The runway is shorter than most current modernization plans assume.

This is the first reason AI-assisted modernization is not optional. It is workforce-critical. The institutional knowledge embedded in 30-year-old COBOL is in the heads of people who will not be working in five years. Encoding that knowledge into testable, modernizable artifacts is a race against retirement.

Regulatory forcing functions

Brazilian enterprises face specific regulatory deadlines that transform modernization from a strategic option into a compliance requirement.

  • BACEN 2028. The Central Bank of Brazil set 2028 as the target for banking modernization. Modern technology controls, API-based open banking interfaces, and enhanced cybersecurity that mainframe platforms cannot deliver natively.
  • LGPD (Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados). Brazil's data protection law requires data governance capabilities (consent management, data lineage, right to erasure) that mainframe architectures were not designed to support.
  • SOX, ISAE 3402, PCI-DSS. International compliance standards expecting modern security controls, encryption practices, and audit mechanisms that legacy platforms struggle to provide.

These deadlines create a 24-36 month window for enterprise modernization. Agentic AI acceleration is essential to meet compliance targets without big-bang risk.

How AI flips the cost equation

The traditional cost structure of COBOL modernization placed the highest expense on understanding, reverse-engineering business rules embedded in decades-old code written by developers who long since retired. This understanding phase consumed 40-60% of project budgets and was the primary bottleneck that caused projects to fail or extend indefinitely.

AI fundamentally inverts this equation. Claude Opus 4.6 can process up to 1 million tokens in a single context window, enough to analyze an entire COBOL application with all its copybooks, JCL, and data definitions simultaneously. What previously took a team of COBOL experts weeks to document can now be completed in hours with AI-generated analysis validated by human experts.

Industry data

Gartner G00833880 identifies 5-20% of operational cost as addressable via AI optimization in legacy systems. Gartner G00842103 projects that agentic AI will reduce the cost-to-value gap by 50% by 2027. Gartner G00835157 shows 61% of enterprises are investing in agentic AI as of June 2025.

Monday morning

If your modernization roadmap still treats AI as "we will pilot in 2027", the runway already expired. The teams compounding now are in production this quarter. Five concrete actions for the first two weeks are listed in Chapter 12.

02FRAMEWORK · CHAPTER 02

Gartner's 5R modernization framework

Five strategies, ranked by transformation depth and risk. The discipline is not picking the right one. It is applying each to the right tier of the estate.

The five Rs

RStrategyEffortValueWhen
R01RetainLowLowStable, low-risk, end-of-life systems
R02RehostLow-MedMediumCloud cost optimization, infra modernization
R03RefactorMediumMed-HighCOBOL → Java/.NET, AI sweet spot
R04RearchitectHighHighStrategic transformation, new boundaries
R05ReplaceVery HighVery HighSaaS exists, or strategic exit

The portfolio approach

No single R fits all workloads. The successful modernization is a portfolio: classify the estate, apply the right pattern per tier, sequence the work so value compounds early.

A typical portfolio for a Brazilian commercial bank with 500M lines of COBOL might land roughly as: 30% R01 Retain (stable batch jobs), 25% R02 Rehost (cost-driven migrations to mainframe-on-cloud), 30% R03 Refactor (the AI sweet spot, customer-facing systems), 10% R04 Rearchitect (open banking APIs, real-time payments), 5% R05 Replace (commodity HR/finance going to SaaS).

Gartner's 2030 prediction

By 2030, over 75% of enterprises using IBM mainframes will adopt low-risk modernization strategies that prioritize incremental transformation over big-bang replacements.

The "low-risk" framing is doing a lot of work here. It means: strangler fig over single cutover, R02/R03 over R05, multi-year coexistence over 18-month rip-and-replace.

Critical insight: GenAI scope

Gartner G00822567

Current GenAI tools excel at code translation and framework refactoring but explicitly exclude architecture modernization from their scope. Simple translation creates "JOBOL", COBOL logic implemented in Java, which represents insufficient modernization.

Effective GenAI-assisted modernization requires combining general-purpose LLMs, specialist domain tools, and rule-based systems. Run a rearchitect pass before translation. AI proposes target boundaries; humans approve them. Then translate to the new shape, not the old one.

04ASSESSMENT · CHAPTER 04

The ten-dimension assessment framework

Before any modernization engagement begins, ten dimensions must be assessed. Skipping this is the most reliable way to convert a budget into a stalled project.

The ten dimensions

  1. Business strategy and modernization drivers. What forcing function is on the table? Cost, risk, regulation, capability gap, competitive pressure?
  2. Financial assessment. ROI model, TCO baseline, value realization timeline. Includes the AI cost-equation flip from Chapter 1.
  3. Technical portfolio assessment. CAST analysis (or equivalent) plus AI-driven complexity scoring. Maps applications to modernization tiers.
  4. Gartner TIME classification and 7R disposition. Tolerate, Invest, Migrate, Eliminate. Combined with the 5R framework from Chapter 2.
  5. People, knowledge, and tribal expertise risk. Who knows what? Who is leaving? What knowledge is uncodified?
  6. Organizational readiness and change management. Has the team done agentic AI before? What is the maturity baseline?
  7. Data governance and architecture. Where does master data live? What does LGPD require that mainframe cannot provide?
  8. Regulatory compliance and risk. SOX, ISAE 3402, PCI-DSS, BACEN, LGPD, sector-specific obligations.
  9. Operational baseline and performance requirements. SMF data: CPU, I/O, response times, batch windows. The yardstick the modernized system must match.
  10. Vendor and technology ecosystem. Existing partner contracts, ISV dependencies, SaaS exits, multi-cloud constraints.

The Go / No-Go gate

The assessment produces a Go/No-Go decision. The No-Go criteria are explicit:

  • No executive sponsor accountable for the outcome
  • No funding committed beyond Wave 1
  • No production telemetry baseline (SMF) available
  • Tribal knowledge concentrated in one person and that person is leaving
  • Compliance gates not pre-cleared with the regulator

If any of these is true, the answer is No-Go. Fix the precondition, then reassess. Modernization initiated without these in place fails 100% of the time.

How agentic AI accelerates the assessment

The Discovery agent (Chapter 7) automates roughly 40-60% of the assessment work: codebase scanning, dependency mapping, complexity scoring, business rule cataloging. What used to be a six-week consulting engagement becomes a two-week effort with a higher-fidelity output.

The remaining 40-60% is human work: business strategy alignment, stakeholder interviews, organizational readiness, compliance interpretation. These are not automatable in 2026 and should not be attempted.

05AI ADVANTAGE · CHAPTER 05

The AI advantage and its limits

Agentic AI brings four capabilities that flip the cost equation. It does NOT replace six categories of human judgment. The 70/30 operating model is the default.

Four AI capabilities that change the game

  1. Automated exploration and discovery. Claude Code maps dependencies across thousands of files, identifies program entry points, traces execution paths, and surfaces implicit couplings that even experienced developers may miss. It reads the entire codebase, not just the files someone remembered to document.
  2. Risk analysis and opportunity mapping. AI evaluates each component's complexity, coupling, test coverage, and business criticality to produce a prioritized modernization roadmap. This replaces subjective expert judgment with data-driven assessment.
  3. Strategic planning with expert oversight. AI generates modernization plans (target architectures, migration sequences, resource estimates) that human architects review and approve. The human-in-the-loop ensures strategic decisions remain under expert control.
  4. Incremental implementation with validation. AI assists with code translation, test generation, and documentation, one component at a time, with validation at each step. Not a black-box conversion. Every output is reviewable and reversible.

What AI does NOT replace

Six decisions remain in human hands:

  1. Final approval of target architecture decisions. AI proposes; humans choose.
  2. Validation of business rule preservation. Subject matter experts certify the new code reproduces the legacy behavior.
  3. Performance tuning for specific workload patterns. Production telemetry beats AI projection.
  4. Production cutover decisions. A human signs the go-live and accepts the rollback plan.
  5. Regulatory compliance sign-off. The accountable executive owns the audit trail.
  6. Escalation handling for edge cases. When AI cannot resolve, route to a domain expert.

The 70/30 operating model

The target operating model is approximately 70-80% AI-executed with 20-30% human oversight, validation, and decision-making, consistent with Gartner's AI-assisted modernization productivity estimates.

IDC US54300026

Only 45% of AI projects deliver measurable business value. The framework in this playbook, with assessment gates, cost ceilings, and governance, is what gets you into that 45%.

06ARCHITECTURE · CHAPTER 06

The five-layer reference architecture

Five layers, top to bottom: Core, Agents, MCP Tools, Platform and Security, Perimeter. Each layer builds on the one below. Five principles govern the whole stack.

Layer 1: Core (developer interface)

Developers interact with Claude models through GitHub Copilot Agent Mode in VS Code, JetBrains, or the GitHub web interface. The model picker allows selecting Claude Opus 4.6 or Sonnet 4.6 for specific tasks.

For power users and automation, Claude Code CLI provides a terminal-based interface with MCP integration. The GitHub Copilot SDK enables building specialized interfaces tailored to modernization workflows.

Configuration files in the repository root, AGENTS.md and CLAUDE.md, provide persistent instructions to agents about project conventions, architecture, and modernization rules.

Layer 2: Agents (multi-model orchestration)

The agent layer implements the intelligence of the modernization pipeline. The multi-agent architecture is validated by Gartner research [G00822558 Figure 3], which identifies a five-agent workflow pattern: Specification → Architecture → Code Generation → Verification → Deployment.

  • Orchestrator (Claude Opus 4.6). The primary reasoning engine. Receives high-level tasks, decomposes them into subtasks, assigns to workers, consolidates results. Uses the full 1M token context window.
  • Worker agents (Claude Sonnet 4.6). Execute specific tasks: code translation, test generation, documentation writing, PR creation. Each worker has focused scope and a clear input/output contract.
  • Scanner agents (Claude Haiku 4.5). Handle high-volume, low-complexity tasks: syntax validation, pattern matching, triage classification, CI/CD checks. Optimized for speed and cost.

Orchestration is managed by Microsoft Semantic Kernel using built-in agent patterns: Sequential (step-by-step), Handoff (agent-to-agent delegation), and Group Chat (collaborative reasoning).

Layer 3: MCP Tools (enterprise connectors)

The Model Context Protocol (MCP) provides a standardized way for agents to connect to enterprise systems. Eight MCP servers form the integration backbone (detailed in Chapter 8).

Layer 4: Platform and Security

  • Azure AI Foundry hosts Claude models via serverless deployment. Provides Model Router, Foundry IQ (dynamic RAG), and Fleet Management.
  • Microsoft Entra ID is the identity provider for all agents and users. RBAC policies control resource access.
  • Azure Key Vault stores all secrets, API keys, and certificates. Agents retrieve credentials through the Vault MCP server.
  • Azure Monitor centralizes logging, metrics, and alerting. Every agent action is logged with structured metadata.
  • Azure Policy and Defender for Cloud enforce compliance and manage security posture.

Layer 5: Perimeter (external interfaces)

  • Customer mainframe. Source COBOL accessed via secure file transfer, mainframe API adapters, or Git mirrors
  • Target Azure services. The modernized application destination
  • External APIs. Third-party integrations the modernized system continues to depend on

Five principles that govern the stack

  1. Multi-model by design. Opus analyzes, Sonnet translates, Haiku scans. Model Router optimizes cost automatically.
  2. Human-in-the-loop at decision points. Agents execute autonomously for routine tasks but pause at approval gates.
  3. Enterprise governance first. Every agent action is logged, permissioned, and auditable.
  4. Incremental and reversible. Every step produces a reviewable artifact. Every deployment can roll back.
  5. Platform-native integration. Uses GitHub and Azure native services rather than custom infrastructure.

Modernization-specific use cases

09OPERATIONAL · CHAPTER 09

Application carving

The hardest problem in COBOL modernization is not translation. It is figuring out how to slice a monolithic estate into independently modernizable units. Carving is the discipline that determines whether the project ships.

Why carving is the hard part

A typical COBOL estate is interconnected in ways that the documentation never captured. CALL chains span dozens of programs, copybooks are shared across modules, JCL jobs cascade. Everything is connected; the question is which connections can be cut and where new contracts can be drawn.

Big-bang attempts fail here. Picking modules to translate without understanding the dependency surface produces translated artifacts that cannot run independently. The carved units must hold their own when traffic shifts to them in Phase 5.

Tiered dependency difficulty model

  • Tier 1, leaf modules. Low coupling, no callers depending on internal state. Easy to carve first. Most utilities, formatters, validators land here.
  • Tier 2, intermediate modules. Some shared state with callers. Moderate difficulty. Domain services for specific business capabilities (loan origination, claim adjudication) typically land here.
  • Tier 3, core modules. High coupling. Save for last. Core ledger, account management, regulatory reporting are typical Tier 3. Translating these first guarantees rework when Tier 1 and 2 surface incompatible assumptions.

SMF runtime behavior analysis

System Management Facility (SMF) records on z/OS contain runtime behavior that static code analysis misses: which programs are actually called in production, how often, with what data shape, in what time windows. SMF data informs the slicing order: heavy real-time programs may need different patterns than batch jobs that run weekly.

Automated discovery with Claude Opus

Run Claude Opus 4.6 against the entire codebase with the Discovery agent (Chapter 7). The 1M-token context window allows Opus to load every COBOL program, copybook, and JCL job simultaneously. Output is a dependency graph with edges weighted by SMF call frequency and complexity scores per module.

Use this output to draft the carving plan: which modules carve first, which later, where the new service boundaries land, and what API contracts the modernized units expose.

Anti-pattern

Carving without SMF data. Static-only analysis suggests boundaries that look clean but disagree with how the system actually behaves under production load. The first time a high-frequency cross-boundary call surfaces in shadow traffic, the architecture has to be redrawn.

10OPERATIONAL · CHAPTER 10

Performance, coexistence, testing

Three challenges that determine production success: matching mainframe performance, running both systems together for 12-24 months, and validating behavioral equivalence with shadow traffic.

Performance assurance

Mainframes process workloads with characteristics that distributed systems do not naturally match: sustained throughput on batch jobs measured in millions of records per minute, sub-second response for transactional banking, predictable latency under load.

Performance assurance has four steps:

  1. Establish the baseline. SMF data captures CPU, I/O, and response time distributions for every modernization target.
  2. Load test with production-like volume. Generate traffic that matches real production patterns. Synthetic load that hits a mean response time hides the tail latency that matters.
  3. Profile hot paths. Use Application Insights and Azure Monitor to identify the 5% of code paths consuming 80% of CPU. Optimize those first.
  4. Benchmark vs mainframe. No regression beyond defined SLOs. If response time degrades, capacity expands, or throughput drops, the cutover gate stays closed.

Coexistence (the strangler fig pattern)

The strangler fig metaphor: a vine grows around an old tree, gradually taking over until the tree dies. Modernization works the same way. The mainframe remains the source of truth while modern services take over functionality, one capability at a time.

Concrete pattern:

  • Router (CDC + feature flags). Routes traffic by percentage rollout, customer ID, region, or business rule. Starts at 10% to modern, 90% to legacy.
  • CDC bidirectional sync. Change Data Capture keeps both systems in sync. No double writes, no eventual consistency surprises.
  • Idempotent modern services. Every operation can be retried safely. State changes are reversible.
  • Gradual traffic migration. 10% → 30% → 50% → 80% → 100%. Each step validated by metrics before advancing.

Coexistence typically runs 12-24 months for a major application. Compressing it shorter than 12 months is the most common cause of cutover failure.

Testing strategy: shadow traffic and behavioral equivalence

  1. Shadow traffic. Production requests are duplicated to the modernized service in read-only mode. Responses are compared, not used. Identifies divergences before they affect users.
  2. Behavioral equivalence tests. Generated by the Test agent during translation. Validate that modernized code produces identical outputs to the original COBOL across thousands of input fixtures.
  3. Mutation testing. Inject defects into the modernized code, confirm tests fail. Validates test suite quality, not just code correctness.
  4. Edge case mining. Discovery agent reads decades of audit logs, surfaces rare scenarios. Generated test fixtures cover them explicitly.
Anti-pattern

Test rot. Tests pass but the code is wrong because tests inherit the same bugs as the translation. Mitigation: generate tests from the original COBOL behavior, not from the translated code. Mutation-test the suite to catch tests that always pass.

11GOVERNANCE · CHAPTER 11

Governance framework

Auditability is not a feature. It is the difference between a pilot and production. The governance framework is what gets the project past compliance review.

Five governance pillars

  1. Every agent action logged with structured metadata. Azure Monitor captures who, when, what, why, and what changed. The audit trail is the side effect of normal operation, not a separate workstream.
  2. Approval gates at decision points. Architecture decisions, production deployments, and schema changes pause for human sign-off. The accountable executive owns each gate.
  3. RBAC at every layer. Microsoft Entra ID controls which agents can read which resources. Key Vault holds all secrets. No agent stores credentials directly.
  4. Cost ceilings per workflow. Token spend capped per agent invocation. Runaways trigger circuit breakers and alert the platform team.
  5. Compliance evidence generated automatically. SOX, ISAE 3402, LGPD, PCI-DSS audit trails as a side effect of normal operation, not a separate compliance project.

Risk categories

RiskMitigation
Cost runawayPer-module cost ceilings; Wave 1/2/3 budget gates; circuit breakers on token spend
Agent anarchySingle Orchestrator; structured action log; approval gates at decision points
Vendor lock-inStandard idiomatic target code; no proprietary SDK injection; multi-model routing
Regulatory non-complianceCompliance gates pre-cleared with regulator; audit trail automated; signed evidence

AgentOps maturity

AgentOps is the operational discipline of running agents in production at scale. Maturity levels:

  • Level 0. Ad-hoc agent invocations. No structured logging. No cost tracking.
  • Level 1. Logging and cost tracking exist but are reviewed manually after the fact.
  • Level 2. Real-time dashboards, alerts on cost runaway and error rates, structured audit log.
  • Level 3. Automated remediation: circuit breakers, fallback paths, hot-swappable models, compliance evidence pipelines.
  • Level 4. Continuous improvement: agent prompts versioned and A/B tested, model performance tracked, cost-per-LOC trended over quarters.

Most enterprises starting modernization sit at Level 0-1. Production cutover gates require Level 2 minimum. Factory model (Chapter 12) requires Level 3.

12EXECUTION · CHAPTER 12

Monday morning

Five concrete actions for the next two weeks. After Wave 1 ships, the next 100 modules become factory work.

Five actions for the first two weeks

  1. Pick one COBOL module under 5K LOC with low business criticality. Not the most important system. The most learnable. This is your Wave 1 quick win.
  2. Stand up GitHub Copilot + Claude on Azure AI Foundry. Provision the Foundry workspace, deploy Claude Opus 4.6 and Sonnet 4.6, connect via Model Router.
  3. Run the Discovery agent on the chosen module. Output: dependency graph, business rule catalog, complexity score. Compare against your team's mental model.
  4. Generate translation + tests for one leaf module. Sonnet 4.6 with behavioral equivalence test generation. Review the PR with senior engineers.
  5. Establish governance baseline. Approval gates, cost ceilings, audit logging. Wave 2 cannot start without this.

Two-week target

At the end of Wave 1, you have a translated module with passing behavioral tests, a documented governance plan, and a credible Wave 2 budget. That is what you bring to the steering committee. Not a slide deck. The actual artifact.

The factory model

After Module 1, the next 100 are repeatable. The factory model is what compounds:

  • Reusable carving rules, agent prompts, test fixtures
  • Standardized PR template, review checklist
  • Pre-built CI/CD pipeline per target language
  • Cost predictability: token spend per LOC measured and tracked
  • Throughput: 50K-500K LOC per quarter sustained at steady state

What success looks like at 12 months

  • Throughput of 200K-500K LOC modernized per quarter at steady state
  • Time to first production of 90-180 days, vs industry median of 9-18 months
  • Test coverage of 85%+ minimum, with 100% on critical business rule paths
  • Cost predictability of ±15% token cost per LOC variance band
  • Compliance evidence generated continuously, not as a separate audit project
  • Knowledge codified from tribal expertise into AGENTS.md, skills, and prompts
