## 1. Purpose and Positioning

### 1.1 Why This Document Exists

Every failed mainframe modernization shares a common root cause: insufficient assessment. Organizations jump to technology decisions (which tool, which cloud, which migration pattern) without first understanding what they have, why they want to change, what it will cost, and whether the organization is ready. The result is predictable, scope creep, budget overruns, stalled migrations, and executive disillusionment.

This framework defines the **ten dimensions** that must be evaluated before any modernization execution begins. It synthesizes guidance from Gartner (TIME model, 7Rs framework), Forrester (five modernization best practices), IDC (MarketScape 2025-2026 vendor assessment), and field experience with LATAM financial institutions to create a single, repeatable assessment playbook.

The framework is designed to be technology-agnostic in its evaluation but technology-informed in its recommendations, it works whether the customer ultimately chooses our agentic platform, a competitor offering, or a hybrid approach.

### 1.2 Where It Fits in the Document Series

This assessment framework is the **prerequisite gate** for the entire strategy series. The logical execution sequence is:

| Order | Document | Purpose |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **1st** | **Doc 04: Assessment Framework** (this document) | Evaluate readiness across 10 dimensions |
| 2nd | Doc 05: Strategy & Business Case | Build executive case based on assessment findings |
| 3rd | Doc 06: Technical Architecture | Design agentic framework aligned with assessment |
| 4th | Doc 07: Operational Playbook | Plan carving, performance, coexistence based on baselines |
| 5th | Doc 08: Customer FAQ | Address customer concerns surfaced during assessment |

The assessment generates the inputs that Docs 01-04 consume. Without it, those documents are theoretical; with it, they are grounded in the customer's reality.

### 1.3 Assessment Duration and Governance

| Portfolio Size | Typical Duration | Team Composition |
|---------------|------------------|------------------|
| Small (< 500 COBOL programs) | 4-6 weeks | 1 lead architect, 2 analysts, 1 business analyst |
| Medium (500-5,000 programs) | 8-12 weeks | 1 lead architect, 4 analysts, 2 business analysts, 1 data specialist |
| Large (> 5,000 programs) | 12-20 weeks | 2 lead architects, 6-8 analysts, 3 business analysts, 2 data specialists, 1 program manager |

Assessment governance requires an **executive sponsor** (minimum VP-level) and a **steering committee** with representation from IT, business units, finance, risk/compliance, and HR. Weekly steering committee reviews ensure alignment and accelerate decision-making.

## 2. Assessment Framework Overview

### 2.1 The Ten Dimensions

The framework evaluates modernization readiness across ten interconnected dimensions. Each dimension produces specific deliverables that feed the go/no-go decision gate.

| # | Dimension | Key Question | Primary Owner |
|---|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| 1 | Business Strategy & Drivers | Why modernize now? What business outcomes justify the investment? | CIO / CTO |
| 2 | Financial Assessment (ROI/TCO) | What is the current cost, projected cost, and return timeline? | CFO / Finance |
| 3 | Technical Portfolio | What do we have? How complex is it? What are the dependencies? | Enterprise Architecture |
| 4 | TIME/7R Classification | What should happen to each application, migrate, retain, retire, or replace? | Architecture + Business |
| 5 | People & Knowledge Risk | Who knows what? What happens when they leave? How fast is knowledge eroding? | HR + IT Leadership |
| 6 | Organizational Readiness | Is the organization prepared for the change? What gaps exist? | CTO + HR |
| 7 | Data Governance & Architecture | Where is the data? Who owns it? How does it move? What are the quality issues? | CDO / Data Architecture |
| 8 | Regulatory & Compliance Risk | What regulations constrain the migration? What audit requirements exist? | CISO / Legal / Compliance |
| 9 | Operational Baseline | What are the current SLAs, batch windows, peak loads, and performance thresholds? | IT Operations |
| 10 | Vendor & Technology Ecosystem | What tools, partners, and platforms are available? What is the commercial model? | Procurement + Architecture |

### 2.2 Assessment Workflow

The assessment proceeds in three phases:

**Phase A, Discovery (Weeks 1-4).** Gather data across all ten dimensions simultaneously. This phase is about collecting facts, not making decisions. CAST runs the technical portfolio scan. Business analysts interview stakeholders. Finance collects cost data. HR maps the skills landscape.

**Phase B, Analysis (Weeks 3-6, overlapping with Phase A).** Analyze collected data, classify applications using TIME/7R, build the financial model, score organizational readiness, and identify interdependencies between dimensions.

**Phase C, Decision (Weeks 5-8).** Consolidate findings into the Assessment Report. Present recommendations to the steering committee. Execute the go/no-go gate. If "go," feed findings into Docs 01-04 for execution planning.

### 2.3 Scoring and Readiness Classification

Each dimension is scored on a 1-5 readiness scale:

| Score | Classification | Meaning |
|-------|---------------|---------|
| 5 | Ready | Proceed with full modernization |
| 4 | Ready with conditions | Proceed after addressing minor gaps |
| 3 | Conditionally ready | Proceed only after specific remediation (3-6 months) |
| 2 | Not ready | Significant gaps must be resolved before modernization |
| 1 | Blocked | Fundamental blockers exist, modernization should not start |

The **overall readiness score** is the weighted average across dimensions, with Business Strategy (15%), Financial Assessment (15%), and People & Knowledge Risk (15%) weighted highest, because modernization fails most often from business misalignment, funding shortfalls, and knowledge loss, not from technical limitations.

| Dimension | Weight |
|-----------|--------|
| Business Strategy & Drivers | 15% |
| Financial Assessment | 15% |
| People & Knowledge Risk | 15% |
| Technical Portfolio | 10% |
| TIME/7R Classification | 10% |
| Organizational Readiness | 10% |
| Data Governance | 8% |
| Regulatory & Compliance | 7% |
| Operational Baseline | 5% |
| Vendor Ecosystem | 5% |

**Go/No-Go thresholds:** Overall score ≥ 3.5 = Proceed. Score 2.5-3.4 = Remediate first. Score < 2.5 = Do not proceed until fundamentals are addressed.

## 3. Dimension 1: Business Strategy and Modernization Drivers

### Objective

Establish a clear, executive-sponsored business rationale for modernization. Without strong business drivers, modernization becomes an IT project without business commitment, and IT projects without business commitment get defunded at the first budget review.

### Key Questions

1. What are the top 3 business outcomes the organization expects from modernization?
2. Is modernization driven by cost reduction, revenue enablement, risk mitigation, or competitive pressure?
3. Has the board or C-suite explicitly endorsed a modernization initiative?
4. What is the time horizon for expected results, 12 months, 24 months, or 36+ months?
5. Are there external deadlines (regulatory, contractual, vendor end-of-support) creating urgency?
6. What happens if the organization does nothing, what is the cost of inaction?

### Common Business Drivers

| Driver | Description | Urgency Indicator |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **MIPS Cost Escalation** | Mainframe software costs growing 5-8% annually, consuming IT budget | High, immediate financial pressure |
| **Talent Cliff** | Average COBOL developer age > 55; retirement wave in 3-5 years | Critical, irreversible knowledge loss |
| **Time-to-Market** | Mainframe release cycles measured in months vs. competitor weeks | High, competitive disadvantage |
| **Digital Products** | Inability to launch mobile, API-first, or AI-powered products on mainframe | Medium-High, strategic opportunity cost |
| **Regulatory Pressure** | New regulations require capabilities the mainframe cannot support | High, compliance deadlines are non-negotiable |
| **M&A Integration** | Post-acquisition system consolidation requires modern, flexible architectures | Medium, driven by deal timeline |
| **Vendor Risk** | Dependence on single vendor (IBM) for pricing, roadmap, support | Medium, strategic optionality concern |

### Deliverables

1. **Business Case One-Pager**, Executive summary of drivers, expected outcomes, and cost of inaction
2. **Stakeholder Map**, Who sponsors, who decides, who influences, who blocks
3. **Driver Priority Matrix**, Ranked drivers with urgency, impact, and evidence
4. **Cost of Inaction Model**, What happens financially and operationally if modernization is deferred 1, 3, and 5 years

### Scoring Criteria

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Board-approved initiative with funded budget and dedicated team |
| 4 | C-suite sponsor with budget allocation in progress |
| 3 | IT leadership recognizes the need; business alignment is partial |
| 2 | Modernization is discussed but lacks executive sponsorship |
| 1 | No executive awareness or interest in modernization |

## 4. Dimension 2: Financial Assessment (ROI, TCO, Value Realization)

### Objective

Build a rigorous financial model that quantifies current costs, projected migration investment, expected savings, revenue enablement, and payback period. This dimension answers the CFO's question: "What does it cost, what do we get, and when do we get it?"

### Key Questions

1. What is the current annual mainframe TCO (MIPS, licenses, facilities, personnel, support)?
2. What percentage of the IT budget goes to mainframe maintenance vs. innovation?
3. What is the projected Azure cloud TCO for the migrated workloads?
4. What is the total migration investment (tooling, services, training, parallel operations)?
5. What is the expected payback period and IRR?
6. What non-financial value (agility, risk reduction, talent access) should be quantified?

### Financial Model Structure

The financial model must capture four cost categories:

**Current State Costs (Annual Mainframe TCO)**

| Cost Category | Typical Range | Data Source |
|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| MIPS/MSU charges | 40-55% of mainframe TCO | IBM contract, SCRT reports |
| Software licenses (DB2, CICS, MQ, IMS) | 15-25% | License inventory |
| Facilities (data center, power, cooling) | 5-10% | Facilities management |
| Personnel (operators, sys programmers, COBOL developers) | 15-25% | HR compensation data |
| Third-party support and services | 5-10% | Procurement records |

**Migration Investment (One-Time)**

| Cost Category | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------------|---------------|-------|
| Assessment and planning | 5-8% of total migration cost | This framework, CAST licensing |
| Migration tooling and platform | 10-15% | Azure AI Foundry, GitHub, Claude API |
| Professional services (internal + partner) | 40-50% | Largest cost, skills-intensive |
| Testing and validation | 15-20% | Often underestimated, budget generously |
| Training and upskilling | 5-8% | Cloud, DevOps, AI/ML skills |
| Contingency reserve | 15-20% | Non-negotiable buffer for unknowns |

**Target State Costs (Annual Cloud TCO)**

| Cost Category | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------------|---------------|-------|
| Azure compute and storage | Variable, based on workload | Use Azure Pricing Calculator |
| Software licenses (PaaS, SaaS) | Depends on architecture choices | Consumption-based model |
| Personnel (cloud engineers, SREs) | Typically 20-30% lower headcount | Different skills, different cost base |
| Managed services | Optional, based on operational model | Three-tier model per Doc 05, §4.5 |

**Value Realization (Quantified Benefits)**

| Benefit Category | Industry Benchmark | Source |
|-----------------|-------------------|--------|
| Infrastructure cost reduction | 15-35% annual savings | [IBM Research](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/application-modernization) |
| Application maintenance reduction | 30-50% cost decrease | [IBM Research](https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/application-modernization) |
| Release cycle acceleration | 40-60% faster deployment | [Forrester](https://www.forrester.com/report/the-state-of-mainframe-global-2025/RES183032) |
| IT operations productivity | 30% improvement | Industry average |
| Revenue growth differential | +17.3 percentage points above industry average | [MIT CISR](https://cisr.mit.edu/publication/2023_0601_DigitalTransformationLeaders) |
| Security breach risk reduction | 50% decrease | Industry average |
| Three-year ROI | 200-304% | [Gartner](https://www.gartner.com/en/information-technology/insights/application-modernization) |
| Typical payback period | 18-30 months | Industry average for large banks |

### Deliverables

1. **TCO Comparison Model**, Current mainframe TCO vs. projected cloud TCO (5-year horizon)
2. **Migration Investment Estimate**, Total cost by category with confidence ranges
3. **ROI/IRR/Payback Analysis**, Financial returns with sensitivity analysis (best/expected/worst)
4. **Value Realization Roadmap**, When specific financial benefits begin to materialize (by wave)
5. **Cost of Inaction Projection**, Financial trajectory if modernization is deferred

### Scoring Criteria

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Complete TCO model with verified data; ROI > 200%; payback < 18 months |
| 4 | TCO model built with reasonable estimates; ROI > 150%; payback < 24 months |
| 3 | Partial TCO data available; ROI appears positive but not fully modeled |
| 2 | Insufficient cost data; ROI unclear or marginal |
| 1 | No financial data available; business case cannot be substantiated |

## 5. Dimension 3: Technical Portfolio Assessment (CAST + Agentic)

### Objective

Build a complete technical inventory of all mainframe applications, their complexity, dependencies, technology stacks, and integration points. This is where CAST Imaging enters, but it is only one input to a broader technical picture.

### Key Questions

1. How many COBOL programs, copybooks, JCL procedures, and screens exist?
2. What other languages and technologies are present (Natural, PL/I, Assembler, REXX)?
3. What are the database technologies (DB2, IMS, VSAM, Adabas)?
4. What are the transaction processors (CICS, IMS/TM, Natural/Adabas)?
5. What batch schedulers are in use (CA-7, TWS, Control-M)?
6. What are the inter-application dependencies (program calls, data sharing, queue-based)?
7. What percentage of code is dead (never executed in production)?
8. What is the cyclomatic complexity distribution of the portfolio?

### Assessment Approach, Three-Layer Technical Discovery

**Layer 1: Static Code Analysis (CAST Imaging)**

CAST Imaging performs automated scanning of the entire codebase and produces:

- Application inventory with line counts, language breakdown, and complexity metrics
- Dependency graphs showing program-to-program, program-to-data, and program-to-screen relationships
- Dead code identification (programs not called by any other program or JCL)
- Technology stack mapping (which programs use which CICS commands, DB2 calls, MQ APIs)
- Cyclomatic complexity distribution for prioritization
- **Accuracy for dependencies: 60-65%**, captures explicit, compile-time relationships

**Layer 2: Runtime Behavior Analysis (SMF Records)**

SMF (System Management Facilities) records capture actual runtime behavior that static analysis cannot see. This addresses the 35-40% of dependencies that CAST misses:

| SMF Type | What It Captures | Modernization Value |
|----------|------------------|---------------------|
| Type 30 | Job-level resource consumption | Batch workload profiling, resource sizing |
| Types 70-79 | CPU, memory, I/O utilization by workload | Performance baseline for SLA validation |
| Type 110 | CICS transaction performance | Online transaction profiling, response time baselines |
| Type 42 | SMF self-description | Record format validation |
| Type 80 | RACF security events | Security policy dependency mapping |

SMF analysis reveals implicit dependencies: programs that share VSAM files without direct calls, batch jobs that must run in sequence due to data dependencies, CICS transactions that trigger downstream batch processes. This is the "dark matter" of mainframe dependencies that sinks migration projects when undetected.

**Layer 3: Expert Interview and Documentation Review**

Neither static analysis nor SMF records capture business context. Expert interviews with mainframe developers, operations staff, and business analysts reveal:

- Business rules encoded only in tribal knowledge (never documented, never in code comments)
- Workarounds and "special cases" that exist in operator run-books
- Seasonal variations (year-end processing, regulatory reporting cycles)
- Political dependencies (applications that "belong" to specific business units)

### Tiered Dependency Difficulty Model

Based on the three-layer discovery, each dependency is classified by difficulty:

| Tier | Difficulty | Discovery Accuracy | Approach |
|------|-----------|-------------------|----------|
| **Tier 1** | Easy, Direct program CALLs, explicit DB2 queries | 95-100% | CAST automated detection |
| **Tier 2** | Moderate, Shared copybooks, common DB2 tables, MQ queues | 80-90% | CAST + dependency graph analysis |
| **Tier 3** | Hard, VSAM file sharing, dynamic CALLs, JCL symbolic parameters | 50-70% | SMF runtime analysis + expert review |
| **Tier 4** | Hardest, Implicit timing, operator knowledge, undocumented batch sequences | 35-40% | SMF deep analysis + expert interviews |

### Deliverables

1. **Application Inventory**, Complete list with LOC, language, complexity, last modified date
2. **Dependency Map**, Visual graph of all three tiers of dependencies
3. **Dead Code Report**, Programs eligible for retirement before migration
4. **Technology Stack Matrix**, Technologies used per application (DB2, CICS, MQ, VSAM, etc.)
5. **Complexity Distribution**, Portfolio segmented by low/medium/high/extreme complexity
6. **CAST Assessment Report**, Full CAST Imaging output with analysis commentary

### Scoring Criteria

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Complete 3-layer discovery; all dependencies classified; CAST + SMF + expert data merged |
| 4 | CAST complete + partial SMF analysis; most dependencies identified |
| 3 | CAST complete; SMF not available; expert interviews done |
| 2 | Partial CAST scan; incomplete inventory; major gaps in dependency understanding |
| 1 | No automated scanning; inventory based on manual estimates only |

### Agent Readiness Assessment (Gartner AI Agent Assessment Framework)

**Important:** When rebuilding applications using agentic AI (the "Rebuild" 7R disposition), the portfolio must be assessed against Gartner's AI Agent Assessment Framework (G00827372, G00836131) to determine whether agentic AI is the appropriate tool for specific applications.

#### Six Agent Capabilities Scoring

According to Gartner (G00827372), all AI agent capabilities must be scored on a **5-level scale**:

| Level | Definition | Example |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| **1, Minimal** | No agent functionality; deterministic scripting only | Simple automation/RPA |
| **2, Emerging** | Agent perceives environment; limited autonomy; heavy human guidance | AI Assistant with user approval loops |
| **3, Basic** | Agent perceives, decides, and acts independently within guardrails | Moderately autonomous workflow automation |
| **4, Intermediate** | Agent adapts to environmental changes; manages moderate complexity | Agent handling variable business rules with learning |
| **5, Advanced** | Agent reasons across complex, dynamic scenarios; minimal human intervention | Advanced agentic AI handling edge cases autonomously |

Each application being rebuilt with agentic AI must be assessed on six core capabilities:

| Capability | Definition | Assessment Question |
|------------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Perception** | Agent's ability to sense and interpret the environment (code inputs, data, user requests) | Can the agent effectively interpret code structure, business logic, and data semantics? |
| **Decisioning** | Agent's capacity to evaluate options and make independent choices | Can the agent choose between multiple valid refactoring approaches? |
| **Actioning** | Agent's ability to execute decisions (code generation, testing, deployment) | Can the agent generate correct code, run tests, and validate transformations? |
| **Agency** | Agent's degree of autonomy (ability to operate without human approval) | Can the agent proceed autonomously or does every step need human review? |
| **Adaptability** | Agent's ability to adjust strategy when encountering unexpected conditions | Can the agent handle code variations, undocumented patterns, or exceptions? |
| **Knowledge** | Agent's access to and effective use of domain knowledge (COBOL, business logic, Azure architecture) | Does the agent understand COBOL semantics, mainframe-specific patterns, and cloud target platforms? |

**All six capabilities must be at least "Basic" (level 3) for agentic AI to be viable; missing capabilities indicate the application should use traditional code generation tools.**

#### Gartner Naming Conventions

According to Gartner (G00827372 Table 2), terminology depends on capability levels:

| Terminology | Agent Level | Use Case |
|-------------|------------|----------|
| Do **not** use "agent" or "agentic" terminology | Minimal (1) | Pure RPA or automation; no AI autonomy |
| **"AI Assistant"** | Emerging (2) | Requires human approval for all actions |
| **"AI Agent"** | Basic (3) or higher | Autonomous within defined scope; human oversight |
| **"Agentic AI"** | Emerging (2) and above, **all 6 capabilities** | Fully autonomous agent system with adaptability and knowledge |

#### When NOT to Use Agentic AI (Gartner G00827372)

Before assigning the "Rebuild" 7R disposition with agentic AI, screen for disqualifying factors:

| Blocker | Indicator | Mitigation |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Overkill** | Application environment is stable, goals are static, execution is deterministic | Use traditional refactoring or replatforming instead |
| **Too Complex** | Application requires domain knowledge beyond current AI capabilities, or risk of failure is unacceptable | Use hybrid approach: agentic for analysis, human architects for decisions |
| **Cost Escalation** | Pay-per-use agent services can exceed budget for large-scale rebuilds | Pre-negotiate fixed pricing; use open-source agents where possible |
| **Human Touch Required** | Application requires creativity, nuanced business judgment, or customer empathy | Assign to human architects; use agents for documentation and analysis |
| **Unreliability** | Cascading failures without robust error handling; agent failures propagate downstream | Require comprehensive error handling, testing frameworks, and rollback plans |
| **Real-Time Performance** | Latency-sensitive applications or sub-millisecond requirements | Use traditional code generation; agents are slower |
| **Responsible AI Risk** | Regulatory requirements demand explainability, transparency, or auditability | Ensure agent decisions can be traced and validated by humans |
| **Sustainability** | Environmental concerns about compute cost and energy consumption | Combine agentic analysis with human-optimized implementation |

#### Autonomy Level Decision Framework (Gartner G00822558)

For applications approved for agentic AI rebuilds, determine the appropriate **autonomy level** based on three factors (G00822558, "Innovation Insight for Software Global Black Belting"):

| Factor | Low Autonomy | Medium Autonomy | High Autonomy |
|--------|--------------|-----------------|----------------|
| **Business Criticality** | Non-critical, low revenue impact | Moderate criticality, revenue support | Mission-critical, revenue-generating |
| **Risk Profile** | Low risk, reversible changes | Moderate risk, recovery is complex | High risk, failure is costly |
| **Complexity** | Simple applications, straightforward logic | Moderate interdependencies, moderate complexity | Complex business logic, tight coupling |

**Result:** Low/Low/Low autonomy → AI Assistant with human approval. Medium values → AI Agent with guardrails. High/High/High → Agentic AI with continuous monitoring.

#### Capability × Requirements Assessment Matrix

For each COBOL application targeted for agentic AI rebuild, create a **capability-requirements matrix** (G00836131, "Tool: AI Agent Assessment Framework"):

| Use-Case Requirement | Agent Capability Needed | Minimum Level Required |
|---------------------|------------------------|-----------------------|
| **Complexity of COBOL environment** (monolithic, coupled, undocumented) | Perception + Knowledge | Basic (3) |
| **Complexity of refactoring goals** (preserve logic, migrate to cloud, maintain APIs) | Decisioning + Agency | Basic (3) |
| **Variability of code patterns** (Tier 1-4 dependencies, exceptions) | Adaptability + Perception | Basic (3) |
| **Degree of autonomy needed** (full rebuild vs. human-in-loop) | Agency | Intermediate (4) for full autonomy |
| **Dynamics of legacy environment** (ongoing changes, emerging patterns) | Adaptability + Knowledge | Intermediate (4) if high dynamics |
| **Versatility of multi-language target** (Java, C#, Python, mixed stack) | Decisioning + Knowledge | Advanced (5) if true polyglot |

**Assessment approach:** For each application's use case, list requirements in the left column, then assess whether agent capabilities meet the minimum level. If any capability is below the minimum, escalate to human architects or change the approach (e.g., break into smaller sub-applications).

#### Single vs. Multiagent Systems Decision

According to Gartner (G00827372), determine whether to use a **single agent or multiagent system (MAS)**:

| Factor | Single Agent | Multiagent System |
|--------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Complexity of Environment** | Low-moderate; predictable patterns | High; many variables and edge cases |
| **Complexity of Goals** | Single-purpose rebuilds (e.g., "COBOL to Java") | Multi-stage processes (analysis → design → code → test → validate) |
| **Execution Variability** | Low; similar patterns across portfolio | High; different applications need different strategies |
| **Degree of Autonomy** | High; single agent owns the process | Moderate-High; agents specialize and coordinate |
| **Dynamics of Environment** | Stable; portfolio doesn't change mid-project | Dynamic; portfolio grows, priorities shift, tech evolves |
| **Sophistication Required** | Straightforward refactoring | Complex orchestration: Discovery Agent → Architecture Agent → Code Agent → Test Agent → Deployment Agent |

**Decision rule:** If more than 3 of the factors are in the right column, implement a multiagent system. Single agents fail on high-complexity rebuilds because a single agent cannot excel at discovery, architecture, code generation, testing, and deployment simultaneously.

## 6. Dimension 4: Gartner TIME Classification and 7R Disposition

### Objective

Classify every application in the portfolio using the Gartner TIME model and assign a modernization disposition (7Rs). This dimension transforms the raw technical inventory into an actionable roadmap by answering "what should happen to each application?"

### The TIME Model

Gartner's TIME framework classifies applications along two axes: **technical fit** (code quality, maintainability, architectural compatibility) and **functional fit** (business value, strategic alignment, user satisfaction).

| Quadrant | Technical Fit | Functional Fit | Strategy | Typical % of Portfolio |
|----------|--------------|----------------|----------|----------------------|
| **Tolerate** | High | Low | Maintain as-is; not worth the investment to change | 10-20% |
| **Invest** | High | High | Enhance and expand; these are strategic assets | 15-25% |
| **Migrate** | Low | High | Move to modern platform; business value justifies investment | 30-45% |
| **Eliminate** | Low | Low | Retire and decommission; no business or technical justification | 15-25% |

### The 7Rs Disposition Model

Each application in the "Migrate" quadrant receives a specific modernization disposition:

| Disposition | Description | When to Use | Risk Level |
|-------------|-------------|-------------|------------|
| **Rehost** | Lift-and-shift to Azure (e.g., Micro Focus, Rocket) | High urgency, low complexity, need to exit mainframe fast | Low |
| **Replatform** | Move to Azure with minor optimizations (e.g., DB2 → Azure SQL) | Moderate complexity, clear platform mapping exists | Low-Medium |
| **Refactor** | Restructure COBOL for cloud-native operation without rewriting | Good code quality, need cloud scalability | Medium |
| **Rearchitect** | Decompose monolith into microservices on Azure | High business value, long-term strategic application | Medium-High |
| **Rebuild** | Rewrite in modern language (Java, C#, Python) using agentic AI | Code is unmaintainable, business logic must be preserved | High |
| **Replace** | Substitute with commercial SaaS or packaged software | Commodity function with available off-the-shelf solution | Medium |
| **Retire** | Decommission; no longer needed | Dead code, duplicate function, business process eliminated | Very Low |

### Classification Process

The classification proceeds in four steps:

**Step 1: Technical Fit Scoring.** Use CAST metrics: cyclomatic complexity, technical debt ratio, coupling/cohesion metrics, technology obsolescence, and maintainability index. Each application gets a technical fit score from 1 (poor) to 5 (excellent).

**Step 2: Functional Fit Scoring.** Business analysts score each application based on: business criticality, user satisfaction, strategic alignment, revenue impact, and regulatory importance. Each application gets a functional fit score from 1 to 5.

**Step 3: TIME Quadrant Placement.** Plot each application on the TIME matrix. Applications with scores above the median on both axes go to "Invest." High technical/low functional goes to "Tolerate." Low technical/high functional goes to "Migrate." Low on both goes to "Eliminate."

**Step 4: 7R Assignment for Migrate Quadrant.** For each application in the "Migrate" quadrant, the architecture team assigns a specific 7R disposition based on: code quality, dependency complexity, business criticality, available target platform, and risk tolerance.

### Integration with BIAN/ACORD Domain Mapping

For financial services clients, the TIME/7R classification should be enriched with **industry reference architecture** alignment:

- **Banking:** Map applications to [BIAN](https://bian.org/) Service Domains (300+ standardized domains covering payments, lending, deposits, compliance, etc.). This ensures microservice boundaries follow industry standards, not arbitrary technical decomposition.
- **Insurance:** Map applications to [ACORD](https://www.acord.org/) data model standards for policy administration, claims management, and billing domains.

This domain mapping prevents the common anti-pattern of creating microservices that don't align with how the industry actually operates.

### Deliverables

1. **TIME Classification Matrix**, Every application plotted on the 2×2 matrix with scores
2. **7R Disposition Report**, Specific modernization path for each "Migrate" application
3. **Wave Plan Draft**, Preliminary grouping of applications into migration waves based on dependencies, risk, and business priority
4. **Retirement Candidates List**, Applications in the "Eliminate" quadrant with decommission plan
5. **BIAN/ACORD Domain Map**, For financial services: application-to-domain mapping

### Scoring Criteria

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | 100% of portfolio classified; 7R assigned to all Migrate applications; wave plan drafted |
| 4 | >80% classified; 7R assigned to critical applications; wave plan in progress |
| 3 | >50% classified; major applications have 7R assignment |
| 2 | Classification started but incomplete; many applications unscored |
| 1 | No classification performed |

## 7. Dimension 5: People, Knowledge, and Tribal Expertise Risk

### Objective

Assess the risk of knowledge loss due to workforce retirement, attrition, and the concentration of critical expertise in a small number of individuals. This is frequently the most urgent driver and the most underestimated risk.

### Why This Dimension Carries 15% Weight

The global COBOL workforce is experiencing a structural decline that cannot be reversed:

- Average COBOL developer age exceeds 55 years in most organizations
- Universities have largely eliminated COBOL from curricula
- The pool of experienced mainframe professionals shrinks by an estimated 5-8% annually
- Knowledge transfer takes 12-24 months for complex systems, there is no shortcut
- When a developer with 30 years of system knowledge retires, that knowledge is permanently lost

For LATAM financial institutions, this is even more acute. Many Brazilian banks have COBOL systems that were built in the 1980s and 1990s by developers who are now approaching retirement age. The business rules encoded in these systems, interest calculation methods, regulatory reporting logic, inter-bank settlement procedures, exist nowhere except in the code and in those developers' memories.

### Key Questions

1. How many mainframe specialists (COBOL developers, system programmers, database administrators) does the organization employ?
2. What is the average age and expected retirement timeline for each specialist?
3. How many individuals are "single points of knowledge" for critical systems?
4. Is there a formal knowledge transfer or documentation program in place?
5. What is the annual attrition rate for mainframe specialists?
6. Are there existing documentation gaps, systems with no current documentation?
7. Has the organization attempted to hire or train COBOL developers recently? What was the result?

### Knowledge Concentration Risk Matrix

| Risk Level | Criteria | Action Required |
|-----------|----------|-----------------|
| **Critical** | Only 1 person understands the system; retirement within 2 years | Immediate knowledge capture; accelerate modernization |
| **High** | 2-3 people understand the system; retirement within 3-5 years | Structured knowledge transfer; prioritize for early waves |
| **Medium** | Small team (4-6) with some documented knowledge | Document gaps; include in standard wave planning |
| **Low** | Well-documented system with multiple knowledgeable staff | Standard process; modernize based on business priority |

### Knowledge Capture Strategy

For systems in the "Critical" or "High" risk categories, the assessment must include a knowledge capture plan:

**Structured Expert Interviews.** Record detailed sessions with subject matter experts covering: system purpose, business rules, integration points, known issues, workarounds, seasonal variations, and "things that only I know." These recordings become input for the agentic AI knowledge base.

**Code Annotation Sprints.** Pair retiring experts with junior developers for intensive code review sessions. The expert explains the business logic; the junior developer adds comments and documentation. Our Code Analyzer Agent can accelerate this by generating initial code explanations that the expert validates rather than writes from scratch.

**Shadow Operations.** Junior staff shadow the expert during actual production operations, incident response, batch monitoring, month-end processing, to capture procedural knowledge that never gets documented.

**AI-Assisted Knowledge Extraction.** The Claude-powered Discovery Agent can analyze COBOL code and generate business rule documentation automatically, which experts then validate and correct. This approach is 3-5x faster than manual documentation and captures 70-85% of embedded business logic without expert involvement.

### Agent Autonomy Determination by Knowledge Risk

According to Gartner (G00822558, G00827372), the presence of knowledge concentration risk affects the appropriate **autonomy level** for agentic AI rebuilds of affected applications.

**Decision rule:** If an application is a "single point of knowledge" or in the "Critical" risk category, agent autonomy must be constrained:

| Knowledge Risk Level | Recommended Agent Autonomy | Rationale |
|---------------------|---------------------------|-----------|
| **Critical** (1 expert, retirement <2 years) | **Low autonomy; AI Assistant with heavy human review** | Domain knowledge is too concentrated; human experts must validate every decision |
| **High** (2-3 experts, retirement <5 years) | **Medium autonomy; AI Agent with guardrails** | Limited redundancy; agent can act within guardrails but expert must review architecture |
| **Medium** (4-6 staff, some docs available) | **Medium autonomy; AI Agent with spot checks** | Reasonable knowledge distribution; agent can proceed with periodic expert review |
| **Low** (well-documented, multiple knowledgeable staff) | **High autonomy; Agentic AI with monitoring** | Knowledge is distributed and documented; agent can proceed autonomously with operational monitoring |

Conversely, if **knowledge is being captured and transferred** (through the Knowledge Capture Strategy above), the autonomy level can be raised during the modernization:

- **Baseline:** Start with the autonomy level matching the risk category
- **During Capture:** As experts transfer knowledge and validate automated documentation, raise autonomy level incrementally
- **Post-Migration:** Once documentation is complete and validated, full agentic autonomy is sustainable

This approach ensures that agentic AI doesn't move faster than knowledge can be reliably captured and validated.

### Deliverables

1. **Skills Inventory**, Complete roster of mainframe specialists with skills, experience, and retirement timeline
2. **Knowledge Concentration Map**, Systems plotted by number of experts and retirement urgency
3. **Single-Point-of-Knowledge Report**, Critical systems where one person holds the knowledge
4. **Knowledge Capture Plan**, Specific activities, timeline, and resource requirements
5. **Attrition Impact Model**, Projected cost and risk increase as specialists retire (1, 3, 5 year horizon)

### Scoring Criteria

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 5 | Complete skills inventory; no critical single-points; active knowledge transfer program |
| 4 | Skills inventory done; few single-points identified; knowledge capture planned |
| 3 | Partial skills data; some single-points known; no formal capture program |
| 2 | Limited visibility into skills; known single-points with no mitigation |
| 1 | No skills inventory; likely significant single-points; no knowledge management |

### Forrester's Five Best Practices for Organizational Readiness

Forrester's research identifies five practices that differentiate successful modernization from failed attempts:

1. **Map the value stream before choosing tools.** Understand how work flows from business request to production delivery on the mainframe. This reveals bottlenecks that modernization should address.
2. **Adopt DevOps on the mainframe first.** Organizations that implement CI/CD on the mainframe before migrating off it have 2.3x higher success rates. It builds the muscle memory needed for cloud operations.
3. **Train existing staff rather than replace them.** Mainframe developers who learn cloud technologies are more valuable than cloud developers who don't understand the business logic. Retraining programs show 70% retention rates vs. 30% for external hiring.
4. **Start with integration, not migration.** Connect mainframe to cloud via APIs before moving workloads. This creates immediate business value and builds organizational confidence.
5. **Measure outcomes, not activity.** Track business KPIs (time-to-market, cost per transaction, availability) rather than technical metrics (lines migrated, servers provisioned).

## 9. Dimension 7: Data Governance and Data Architecture

### Objective

Map the complete data landscape of the mainframe environment, where data lives, who owns it, how it flows between systems, what quality issues exist, and what regulatory constraints govern its movement. Data is the single largest source of "gravity" keeping organizations on the mainframe.

### Why Data is the Hidden Blocker

Gartner identifies **data gravity** as the primary reason modernization projects stall: the data is so deeply embedded, so interconnected, and so poorly documented that moving it becomes the hardest part of any migration. Mainframe databases (DB2, IMS, VSAM, Adabas) often contain decades of accumulated data with complex referential integrity rules, undocumented business logic in stored procedures, and implicit dependencies through shared data stores.

### Key Questions

1. What databases exist (DB2, IMS, VSAM, Adabas) and what is the total data volume?
2. Who are the data owners for each major data domain (customer, account, transaction, product)?
3. What data flows exist between mainframe applications? Between mainframe and distributed systems?
4. Are there master data management (MDM) standards in place?
5. What data quality issues are known (duplicates, inconsistencies, missing values)?
6. What data is subject to regulatory requirements (LGPD, BACEN, SOX)?
7. What data retention and archival policies exist?
8. Are there real-time data integration requirements (CDC, streaming) vs. batch-only?

### Data Assessment Areas

| Area | What to Assess | Tools and Methods |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| **Data Inventory** | All databases, files, queues, and data stores | CAST analysis + catalog review |
| **Data Flow Mapping** | How data moves between systems (ETL, CDC, MQ, files) | SMF analysis + integration architecture review |
| **Data Ownership** | Who is accountable for each data domain | Business analyst interviews |
| **Data Quality** | Completeness, accuracy, consistency, timeliness | Data profiling tools + business validation |
| **Data Lineage** | Origin, transformations, and consumption of each data element | Metadata tools + expert knowledge |
| **Regulatory Mapping** | Which data is subject to which regulations | Legal/compliance review |
| **Data Volume and Growth** | Current size and projected growth rates | DBA reports + capacity planning data |

### Migration-Specific Data Concerns

**Referential integrity during coexistence.** When some applications have migrated and others remain on the mainframe, data must stay synchronized. The coexistence patterns in Doc 07 depend on the data architecture assessment to determine which synchronization pattern (CDC, dual-write, event sourcing) is appropriate.

**VSAM to modern database mapping.** VSAM files are the most challenging data migration target because they have no schema, no query language, and their structure is defined entirely in COBOL record layouts. Each VSAM file requires individual analysis to determine the target schema.

**Temporal data challenges.** Mainframe applications often use date formats, calendar conventions, and timezone assumptions that differ from modern systems. The assessment must catalog these variations to prevent data corruption during migration.

4. **Data Quality Assessment**, Profiling results with quality scores per domain
5. **Regulatory Data Map**, Which data is subject to which regulations
6. **Data Migration Complexity Assessment**, Difficulty rating for each data store migration

| 4 | Data inventory mostly complete; major flows mapped; some quality data available |

### Objective

| Metric Category | What to Capture | How to Capture |

| Data flow mapping | 4-8 weeks | 2-3 weeks | Data Discovery Agent analyzing DB2 catalogs, VSAM usage, MQ flows |
| TIME/7R classification (technical scoring) | 4-6 weeks | 1-2 weeks | Automated metrics + classification rules |

The agentic approach reduces total assessment duration by **40-60%** while improving accuracy through automated analysis of data that humans would take months to review manually.

### CAST Integration with Agentic Platform

CAST outputs feed directly into our agentic pipeline:

1. **CAST Imaging** exports the application inventory, dependency graphs, and complexity metrics as structured data
2. The **Discovery Agent** ingests CAST output and enriches it with SMF runtime data (Layer 2) and expert interview data (Layer 3)
3. The **Architecture Agent** uses the enriched inventory to recommend TIME/7R classifications
4. The **Planning Agent** generates wave plans based on dependency graphs and business priority
5. Results are tracked in the **Jira MCP Server** for work item management and progress reporting

This CAST-to-Agentic pipeline is the bridge between traditional assessment tooling and our AI-driven transformation approach.
