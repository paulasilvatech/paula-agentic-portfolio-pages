Introduction

The agent stack most enterprises are building does not exist yet. This is a pattern sourced from the teams who got it working.

6 min read 5 sections

Why another framework

Because the existing ones are for the wrong shape of workload.

Most of what is published as “agentic AI at scale” today is a retrofit of patterns from traditional ML serving, microservice architecture, or data engineering. Those patterns assume predictable compute, static tool contracts, model-file-plus-endpoint security boundaries, and deterministic scheduling. Agents break all four assumptions simultaneously.

The Context Platform Stack is a diagnostic frame, not a product. It names four engineering concerns that every enterprise with agents in production ends up addressing, in a specific order, with a specific dependency structure. Skipping a layer or inverting the order is the most common failure mode I see.

The four layers at a glance

Each chapter that follows answers one diagnostic question for one layer. Layer 01, infrastructure, asks what agents can run on. Layer 02, platform, asks what agents can access and who governs them. Layer 03, context, asks what agents can know. Layer 04, intent, asks what agents should optimize for.

The five chapters map directly: one per layer, plus an integration playbook that covers how to wire them together and what breaks when you do not.

Who this is for

Note
The reader I had in mind

A senior engineer or leader who has shipped at least one agent to production, watched it underdeliver, and wants a vocabulary to explain why. Someone whose CTO is asking for an AI roadmap and whose platform team is already drowning in requests from the first AI experiments.

If you are still at “should we try an agent,” this document is premature. If you are at “our third agent is slower and more expensive than the first,” you are in the right place.

How to use it

Read the introduction, then go directly to the chapter for the layer you are weakest at. Use the maturity self-assessment at the end of chapter 05 to confirm. Do not read all five chapters linearly unless you are setting up the stack from scratch. Most readers need two, maybe three chapters.

Every chapter ends with a short “Monday morning” list, five things you can do this week with no new budget. Those are the cheapest changes with the highest payoff. Everything else is the full program.

What this is not

It is not a tool recommendation. Specific MCP servers, specific orchestration frameworks, specific vector stores come and go faster than an enterprise procurement cycle. The layers themselves are stable.

It is not a cheerleading piece for AI. A recurring theme is the gap between what vendors claim and what production telemetry shows. You will find numbers that are lower than the hype. That is the point.

It is not neutral. This is an opinionated framework, sourced from engagements I have personally led, with patterns I have personally watched succeed or fail. Argue with it.
